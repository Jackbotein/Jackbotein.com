Physical vs chemical changes
Physical changes are shifts in the composition of atoms that are reversible, and able to be undone in full by physical means. By contrast, chemical changes are irreversible shifts in the composition of atoms that cannot be undone by physical means. A common example of a physical change is the broiling of water, where the water undergoes a stage change from liquid to gas, only to return to liquid form. This can be seen when water is broiled, and the water evaporates as a gas and returns to the base of the pan in liquid form. A common chemical change is the burning of a candle, with the candle releasing carbon dioxide into the air during combustion and leaving behind a chemically altered wax remains. This cannot be reversed without chemical means (carbon capture) and is therefore a chemical change.
Demonstrations:
Physical Change: water broiling
Chemical Change: candle combusting
5 Signs of a Physical Change:
#1: Phase Change (eg. change, ice to water to gas)
#2: Color Change
#3: Dissolving
#4: Breaking Apart
#5: Reversible in any way
5 Signs of a Chemical Change:
#1: Unexpected formation of a gas (eg. mix substances, get bubbles)
#2: Unexpected formation of a solid
#3: Unexpected odor (smells like chocolate all of a sudden)
#4: Unexpected change in energy (fire, heat, light)
#5: Unexpected color change (egg becomes white)
Physical Change: doesn't shift atomic compositions
Chemical Change: does shift atomic compositions
